Connections in PoliteDB are stored as **profiles** that you manage from the home screen and open into **workspaces** inside tabs.

This page explains how to think about connection profiles, what information they contain, and how they behave in the app.

## What is a connection profile?

A connection profile is a saved configuration that tells PoliteDB how to reach a particular database. Each profile typically includes:

- **Engine** – for example PostgreSQL, MySQL or Redis.
- **Location** – host, port, and database name (where applicable).
- **Authentication** – username and how to authenticate (password, key, or other methods).
- **Security** – SSL/TLS options, SSH tunnels, and related settings.
- **Metadata** – labels, descriptions, and tags so you can organize profiles by project or environment.

Profiles are reusable: you can open and close them as needed, and they remain available the next time you launch PoliteDB.

## Creating and editing profiles

From the **Connections** view on the home screen, you can:

- Click **New** to create a fresh profile.
- Choose the database engine you want to connect to.
- Fill in the connection details and test them.
- Save the profile so it appears in your list of connections.

You can edit an existing profile at any time to update credentials, hosts, ports, or tags. Edits do not affect any already‑running sessions until you reconnect.

## Opening a connection workspace

When you open a profile, PoliteDB launches a **workspace tab** dedicated to that connection:

- The tab shows the profile name and engine so you can tell connections apart at a glance.
- Within the tab you can:
  - Browse schemas and tables.
  - Open SQL editors.
  - View and export data.
  - Inspect query history.

You can open multiple profiles at once—each in its own tab—and switch between them without losing context.

## Connections and the keychain

PoliteDB is designed so that your profiles and your secrets can be managed separately:

- Profiles keep track of which database to connect to and how.
- Sensitive values such as passwords and keys are stored in the **Keychain** (on macOS) rather than inside the profile itself.

This means you can:

- Safely keep profiles under version control or share them with teammates (where appropriate) without exposing raw credentials.
- Rotate secrets in the keychain without rebuilding every profile from scratch.

For more on how secrets are stored and managed, see the [Keychain guide](/essentials/keychain).
